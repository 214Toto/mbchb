HEMOSTASIS AND BLOOD COAGULATION

Define the term Hemostasis
✓ Hemostasis means stoppage of bleeding and prevention of blood loss when a blood vessel is injured
Hemostasis occurs by four mechanisms, these include ?
✓ Vasoconstriction of the injured vessel
✓ Formation of a platelet plug (temporary hemostatic plug)
✓ Formation of a blood clot (definitive hemostatic plug)
✓ Repair of the damaged blood vessel
Vasoconstriction of the injured vessel occurs as a result of which mechanisms ?
✓ Nervous reflexes (which are initiated by the pain of injury).
✓ Myogenic contraction of the injured vessel (as a direct effect of trauma)
✓ Release of serotonin and thromboxane A2 from platelets (both are V.C).
Explain why a sharply cut vessel bleeds severely as compared to a Bruised vessel.
✓ The greater the trauma, the stronger the V.C and vice versa, Hence a sharly cut vessels experiences little Trauma and thus bleeding more perfussively as compared to a bruised vessel that would have experienced greater trauma
Name at least 5 components that the platelets have a receptor for ?
✓ Collagen
✓ ADP (3 types of receptors)
✓ Thrombopoietin
✓ Fibrinogen
✓ Von Willebrand Factor
Name two sites where vWF is produced
✓ the endothelial cells in the blood vessel walls
✓ the platelets
in the Formation of a Temporary hemostatic plug, explain how Platelet activation Happens .
✓ From injury to blood vessel wall, subendothelial collagen fires are exposed
✓ The platelets adhere to collagen as well as to the vWF in the blood vessel wall via receptors on the platelet membrane, (Platelet Adhesion)
✓ Such binding of platelets initiates platelet activation
What events from platelet activation leads to platelet aggregation
✓ The activated platelets swell and develop pseudopodia, become sticky and release the contents of their granules, which contain ADP
✓ The released ADP binds to the nearby platelets leading to their activation
✓ The newly activated platelets stick to the originally-activated platelets and also release ADP which, in turn activates more and more platelets
Aside ADP, what else promotes Platelet aggregation during the formation of a temporary hemostatic plug ?
✓ Platelet Activation Factor, secreted by the platelets and by the neutrophils and monocytes
✓ Thrombin
✓ thromboxane A2
What is clot retraction ?
✓ it is the shrinking of the cut opening few minutes after formation of a blood clot. (20-60 min)
Grossly, what is expected to be observed during the process of clot retraction
✓ It becomes about one half its original size, and a clear non-coagulable yellow fluid called serum is squeezed out.
✓ As the clot retracts, it pulls the edges of the injured vessle together which helps hemostasis
What is responsible in contracting the clot ?
✓ the platelets themselves contract when they attach to the fibrin threads (leading to clot retraction) due to activation of their contractile proteins (actin, myosin and thrombostenine) by thrombin and Ca2+
What processes leads to the repair of the damaged blood vessel ?
✓ PDGF is released by the platelets, macrophages and endothelial cells
✓ Its Effect is on the Fibroblasts plus the vascular endothelial and smooth muscle cells which grow and proliferate
✓ This growth invades the clot till its organized into the fibrous tissue within 1-2 weeks
How are platelets formed ?
✓ formed as detached bits from giant cells in the bone marrow called megakaryocytes.
What is the half-life and the avarage normal count for Platelets
✓ Their normal count averages 300000/mm3
✓ their half life averages 4 days.
Production of the platelets is regulated by ?
✓ Colony Stimulating Factor
✓ Thrombopoietin
Where is colony Stimulating factor produced and what is its role ?
✓ This factor is produced by the liver
✓ facilitates maturation of the megakaryocytes
What percentage of platelets is found circulating in blood and where is the rest of the percentage residing ?
✓ About 60-75 % of the platelets are present in the circulating blood
✓ while the remainder is present mostly in the spleen
State the structural features of the platelet membrane and their role.
✓ extensive invaginations => contains complex canalicular system which is in contact with the ECF
✓ phospholipids that include platelet factor 3 (which plays an important role in blood clotting).
✓ a coat of glycoprotein on its surface, which favours its adhesion to injured endothelium (but not to normal endothelium)
GIve examples of contractile granules that are found in the cytoplasm of the platelet
✓ actin, myosin and thrombosthenine
WHat does the Dense Granules of the platelet cytoplasm consist of ?
✓ non-protein substances specially ADP and serotonin
The alpha Granules in the cytoplams of a platelets is composed of ?
✓ protein substances (PDGF. Clotting factor XIII , Von Willebrand factor and PAF)
Aside the above mentioned substances, what else is in the cytoplasm ?
✓ Glycogen, lysosomes, mitochondria, residuals of endoplasmic reticulum and Golgi apparatus (which synthesize enzymes and store Ca2+)
what are the primary functions of thromboxane A2 ?
✓ This is a prostaglandin-related substance that causes V.C. and promotes platelet aggregation
Explain how Thromboxane A2 is formed
✓ arachidonic acid is converted to prostaglandin H2 (PGH2) by action of the cyclooxygenase enzyme.
✓ PGH2 is then converted into thromboxane A2 by action of the thromboxane synthetase enzyme.
Explain how Prostacyclin is formed
✓ arachidonic acid is converted to prostaglandin H2 (PGH2) by action of the cyclooxygenase enzyme.
✓ PGH2 is converted into PGI2 (prostacyclin) by action of the prostacyclin synthetase enzyme.
hwo does PGI2 and Thromboxane A2 keep the bllod vessels open and maintaining the blood flow ?
✓ The balance between the actions of thromboxane A2 and prostacyclin normally favours localized platelet aggregation and clot formation.
✓ This prevents excessive extension of the blood clot, and thus keeping the blood vessles open
